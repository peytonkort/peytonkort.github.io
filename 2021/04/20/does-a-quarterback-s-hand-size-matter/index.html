Does a Quarterback's Hand Size Matter?

2021-04-20

One of the best measurements in the NFL Draft Combine is the hand size measurements. Hand size measurement at the NFL Combine is done by measuring the tip of the thumb to the tip of the pinky finger with the hand outstretched.

As a fan this is one of the most laughable measurements in the draft. Who is going to pick there future star Quarterback based off there hand size? So this got me thinking… does hand size really matter? Let’s look at the stats and see.

First I want to narrow my data set down to Quarterbacks that have had a lot of playing time over this season. So I want to filter out with QBs over 1000 passing yards. Also I want to see the mean of hand sizes of all the Quarterbacks that played in the NFL last season. I can use this to make comparison’s down the road.

qbstats <- qbstats %>%
  mutate(FmbG = Fmb/G) %>%
  mutate(IntG = Int/G)
qbstatsmorefiltered <- qbstats %>%
  filter(`Pass Yds` > 1000) %>%
  arrange(desc(`Pass Y/G`))
mean(qbhandsize[["Hand Size"]])

## [1] 0.720395

## [1] 0.4598947
mean(qbstatsmorefiltered[["Pass Y/G"]])
## [1] 238.0821

The statistics I want to compare hand size too is fumbles per game, interceptions per game and passing yards per game. I’m using per game data because some of these players didn’t play every game during the season. This should balance out the comparisons for those who didn’t play a full season.

Hand size should play a part in fumbles, because of the ability to grip the football if you have bigger hands. Therefore you should fumble less than players with smaller hands. Also I’m going to compare interceptions, because they are a part of turnovers just like the fumbles. Lastly I chose passing yards per game because I want to see if hand size has any affect on that stat.

The red and blue lines in this scatter plot shows the average for both hand size and fumbles per game of Quarterbacks in the NFL. You can see that Joe Burrow (2020 #1 overall pick) had the highest fumble per game average, with his small hands of just 9.0 inches. Players like Tom Brady of the Tampa Bay Buccaneers and Patrick Mahomes of the Kansas City Chiefs have below the average hands, and fumbled just below the league average. Dak Prescott of the Dallas Cowboys, who has the biggest hands of the anyone in the data set, had a relatively high fumble per game rate. And Nick Foles of the Chicago Bears who has just over 10.5 inch hands, had a very low fumble per game rate.

This bubble chart gives a great comparison of the top 19 quarterbacks in the NFL, with there hand size and fumbles per game. You can see Matthew Stafford had a 10.0 inch hand (above the 9.55 hand size average of NFL QB’s) and the lowest fumble per game average. Then you look at a guy like Phillip Rivers who has a 9.25 inch hand and has the 2nd lowest fumble per game average. He is a big outlier. Derek Carr of the Las Vegas Raiders had the highest fumble per game average at 0.6875 while having the 3rd smallest hands in this list at 9.125.

Let’s compare some of the quarterback’s with small hands and big hands. The four QB’s I am going to use to compare are Jared Goff (9.0 inch hand), Ryan Tannehill (9.0 inch hand), Russel Wilson (10.25 inch hand) and Josh Allen (10.125 inch hand). All these quarterbacks started 16 games except for Goff who started 15.
